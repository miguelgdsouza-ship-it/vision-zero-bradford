Six-Framework Road Safety Mapping

Key Observations: Adding the RSF Dimension

RSFs are the evidence bridge between the Fatal 5 and the Safe System: the DfT deliberately designed the six RSF sections to align with Safe System pillars, but what makes them operationally powerful is that they capture what police officers actually record at the scene. They translate the abstract Safe System into measurable, attributable data. For VZB, this means every intervention you design under any framework can ultimately be evaluated against RSF trends in Bradford's collision data.

The Speed RSF section is now the largest category in fatal collisions (56%): this is because the RSF definition of "speed" is broader than the old Fatal 4 definition. It now includes loss of control, aggressive/dangerous/reckless driving, and travelling too fast for conditions, not just exceeding the speed limit. This has significant implications for how Bradford reports its speed-related KSI figures and how you frame VZB speed interventions.

RSF "Behaviour or Inexperience" is the most densely connected section: it maps to Safe Road Users, all five Fatal 5 behaviours, Education/Engagement/Enforcement in the E's, and both "Changing Minds" and "Tackling Crime" in the NPCC pillars. Ineffective observation (B4) alone appears in 48-58% of all collisions, making it the single most commonly recorded factor nationally.

The Road and Vehicle RSF sections remain underrecorded: at only 16-22% and 4-6% of collisions respectively. This creates a systematic bias toward user-blame in the evidence base, which is fundamentally at odds with the Safe System principle that the system, not the user, should bear responsibility. VZB should explicitly acknowledge this limitation when using RSF data to inform engineering interventions.

The CF-to-RSF transition creates a data discontinuity: West Yorkshire is recording some data as RSFs with 95% coverage, while the transition is ongoing. Bradford's analysts need to be cautious about comparing pre-2024 CF-mapped data with directly recorded RSF data, as DfT analysis shows significant step changes in recording patterns (particularly a doubling of "overshot junction" and "passing too close" factors).

Safe Road Users remains the most connected node across all six frameworks: it now connects to all five Fatal 5 behaviours, four of the six E's at primary strength, three NPCC pillars, the UN Safer Road Users pillar, and four of the six RSF sections. This confirms it as the convergence point of the entire system, and the strongest argument for cross-directorate ownership within Bradford's governance structure.
